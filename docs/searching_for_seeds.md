## Abstract

The Cross-Sums Compression and Expansion (CRSCE) decompressor reconstructs a $511 \times 511$ binary matrix from four cross-sum projections, cryptographic hash constraints, and four lookup-table partitions (LTPs). Each LTP sub-table is constructed via a Fisher–Yates shuffle seeded by a 64-bit linear congruential generator (LCG) constant, and the choice of seed determines the cell-to-line assignment topology that governs constraint propagation effectiveness. This paper describes the theory of operation and results of `b26c_joint_seed_search.py`, a tool that performs an exhaustive pairwise search over $36 \times 36 = 1{,}296$ candidate seed combinations for LTP sub-tables $T_0$ and $T_1$ while holding sub-tables $T_2$ and $T_3$ fixed. The script addresses a fundamental limitation of the prior greedy sequential search (Appendix B.22): the possibility that independently optimized seeds constitute a local rather than global optimum in the joint seed space. The document formalizes the combinatorial structure of the search, explains the re-compression methodology required for valid evaluation, describes the telemetry-based fitness metric, and reports the experimental outcome. The exhaustive joint search identified the seed pair $(\texttt{CRSCLTPV}, \texttt{CRSCLTPP})$ with a peak depth of 91,090, surpassing the greedy sequential result of 89,331 by 1,759 cells (+1.97%), confirming the presence of significant inter-sub-table interaction effects and demonstrating that coordinate-wise seed optimization is insufficient for this problem (Caldwell, 2026).

## 1. Introduction

In the CRSCE compression scheme, an input bitstream is placed into a Cross-Sum Matrix (CSM) of size $s \times s$ where $s = 511$, and the compressor stores eight families of exact-sum projections together with per-row SHA-1 digests and a block-level SHA-256 digest (Caldwell, 2026). Four of these families are geometric—row sums (LSM), column sums (VSM), diagonal sums (DSM), and anti-diagonal sums (XSM)—and four are pseudorandom lookup-table partitions (LTP1, LTP2, LTP3, LTP4). Decompression is a constraint satisfaction problem (CSP) over 261,121 Boolean variables, solved by a deterministic
depth-first search (DFS) with incremental constraint propagation and cryptographic hash verification at row boundaries.

Each LTP sub-table $T_k$ for $k \in \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$ assigns every cell $(r, c)$ in the $511 \times 511$ matrix to one
of 511 lines, with each line containing exactly 511 cells. The assignment is generated by a Fisher–Yates shuffle of all
$s^2 = 261{,}121$ cell indices, driven by a deterministic LCG seeded with a 64-bit constant. The resulting partition has
no algebraic relationship to the cell coordinates—unlike the geometric families whose line membership is defined by
arithmetic predicates—and this non-linearity is precisely what makes LTPs valuable to the solver (Caldwell, 2026,
Section B.9.3).

The solver's performance, measured as peak DFS depth reached within a fixed time window, depends critically on the LTP
seed values. Experiments conducted during the B.22 research campaign demonstrated a 2.7% depth variance (approximately
2,380 cells) between two seed configurations that were identical in every other respect—same DFS loop, same propagation
engine, same constraint topology of 511 cells per line (Caldwell, 2026, Section B.22.1). This variance arises because
different shuffles produce different cell-to-line assignments, which in turn determine the cross-family line overlap
structure in the plateau band (rows 100–300) where the solver's forcing dead zone activates.

The B.22 search campaign employed a greedy sequential strategy: optimize seed $s_1$ while holding $s_2, s_3, s_4$ at
defaults, then optimize $s_2$ with $s_1$ fixed to its phase-1 winner, and so on through all four sub-tables. This
approach reduces the search space from $K^4$ evaluations (for $K$ candidates per sub-table) to $4K$, but it assumes
that the quality contribution of each sub-table is approximately independent of the others—an assumption that may not
hold when sub-tables interact through shared cells in the constraint propagation cascade. The script
`b26c_joint_seed_search.py` addresses this limitation by performing an exhaustive joint search over all $K^2 = 1{,}296$
combinations of seeds $s_1$ and $s_2$, providing definitive evidence about whether the greedy sequential optimum is also
the joint optimum for the first two sub-tables.

## 2. Background: LTP Construction and Seed Sensitivity

### 2.1 Fisher–Yates LCG Construction

Each LTP sub-table $T_k$ is constructed by the following deterministic procedure. Let $N = s^2 = 261{,}121$ denote the
total number of cells. An array $A$ of all cell indices $(r, c)$ in row-major order is initialized:

```
A = [(0,0), (0,1), ..., (0,510), (1,0), ..., (510,510)]
```

A linear congruential generator (LCG) is seeded with the 64-bit constant $\sigma_k$ and used to drive a Fisher–Yates
shuffle:

```python
for i in range(N - 1, 0, -1):
    j = lcg_next() % (i + 1)
    A[i], A[j] = A[j], A[i]
```

After the shuffle, the first 511 cells in $A$ are assigned to line 0, the next 511 to line 1, and so on:

$$
T_k[A[\ell \cdot s + m]] = \ell, \quad \ell \in \{0, \ldots, s-1\}, \quad m \in \{0, \ldots, s-1\}.
$$

The result is a partition satisfying the three cross-sum partition axioms (Conservation, Uniqueness, Non-repetition)
defined in the CRSCE specification (Caldwell, 2026, Section 2.2.1). Every line contains exactly $s$ cells, every cell
belongs to exactly one line per sub-table, and line sums are encoded in $b = \lceil \log_2(s+1) \rceil = 9$ bits each.

### 2.2 Why Seeds Matter

The LCG seed $\sigma_k$ completely determines the permutation produced by the Fisher–Yates shuffle, and hence the
cell-to-line assignment of sub-table $T_k$. Two different seeds produce two different permutations, yielding partitions
with different structural properties even though both satisfy the same global invariants (511 lines, 511 cells each).

The critical structural property is the cross-family line overlap score $\Phi$, defined in the B.22 specification as the
average number of cells per LTP line that co-reside with nearly tight non-LTP constraint lines in the plateau band:

$$
\Phi = \frac{1}{4 \times 511} \sum_{j=0}^{3} \sum_{k=0}^{510} \Phi(T_j, k)
$$

where $\Phi(T_j, k)$ counts cells on LTP line $k$ of sub-table $T_j$ whose column, diagonal, anti-diagonal, or
cross-sub-table LTP lines have unknown count $u \leq \tau_\Phi$ at the plateau entry depth (Caldwell, 2026, Section
B.22.4). A partition with higher $\Phi$ produces more frequent forcing events in the plateau band because LTP line
residuals reach the forcing thresholds ($\rho = 0$ or $\rho = u$) sooner, triggering cascade propagation into row
completions and SHA-1 verification checks.

The depth variance observed between B.20 and B.25 (approximately 2,380 cells, or 2.7%) is entirely attributable to
different $\Phi$ values arising from different seed configurations. This establishes that seed optimization is a
legitimate avenue for improving solver performance without any algorithmic or architectural changes.

## 3. The Greedy Sequential Search and Its Limitations

### 3.1 B.22 Methodology

The B.22 seed search (implemented in `b22_seed_search.py`) optimizes each sub-table's seed independently in a
four-phase greedy procedure:

```
Phase 1: vary s_1 over K=36 candidates, fix s_2, s_3, s_4 to defaults
Phase 2: vary s_2 over K=36 candidates, fix s_1 to Phase-1 winner, s_3, s_4 to defaults
Phase 3: vary s_3 over K=36 candidates, fix s_1, s_2 to prior winners, s_4 to default
Phase 4: vary s_4 over K=36 candidates, fix s_1, s_2, s_3 to prior winners
```

The candidate seed space consists of 36 values formed by appending each of the ASCII characters `0`–`9` and `A`–`Z` to
the 7-byte prefix `CRSCLTP`, yielding 8-byte (64-bit) seeds of the form:

$$
\sigma = \texttt{0x43\_52\_53\_43\_4C\_54\_50\_}xx
$$

where $xx$ ranges over `0x30`–`0x39` (digits) and `0x41`–`0x5A` (uppercase letters). This yields a compact,
human-readable seed namespace where each seed has a mnemonic label such as `CRSCLTP0`, `CRSCLTPG`, or `CRSCLTPR`.

The total cost is $4 \times 36 = 144$ evaluations rather than $36^4 = 1{,}679{,}616$, a reduction of over four orders
of magnitude. The greedy approach is justified under the assumption that sub-table quality contributions are
approximately separable—that is, the depth gain from an optimal $s_1$ is largely independent of the specific values of
$s_2$, $s_3$, and $s_4$.

### 3.2 The Local Optimum Problem

The separability assumption may fail when two sub-tables interact through constraint propagation cascades. Consider two
LTP sub-tables $T_0$ and $T_1$. A cell $(r, c)$ belongs to one line in $T_0$ and one line in $T_1$. When the solver
assigns a value to $(r, c)$, both lines' residuals are updated simultaneously. If $T_0$-line $\ell_0$ and $T_1$-line
$\ell_1$ happen to share many cells in the plateau band, a forcing event on $\ell_0$ may immediately push $\ell_1$
toward its own forcing threshold, producing a two-step cascade that neither sub-table would achieve alone.

This inter-sub-table coupling means that the optimal $s_1$ may depend on $s_2$, and vice versa. The greedy sequential
search conditions $s_2$'s optimization on a fixed $s_1$ (the Phase-1 winner), but the Phase-1 winner was itself chosen
with $s_2$ fixed to an arbitrary default. If the globally optimal $(s_1^*, s_2^*)$ pair requires a value of $s_1$ that
is suboptimal when $s_2$ is at the default, the greedy search will miss it entirely.

In optimization theory, this phenomenon is well characterized. Coordinate descent—optimizing one variable at a time
while holding others fixed—is guaranteed to converge to a stationary point of a differentiable objective (Wright &
Nocedal, 1999), but the discrete, non-differentiable nature of the DFS depth metric provides no such guarantee. The
"objective function" here is the peak DFS depth $D(\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \sigma_3, \sigma_4)$ over a fixed time window, a
highly non-smooth function of the seed tuple with no known gradient or Lipschitz structure. Coordinate-wise optimization
in discrete combinatorial settings can and does get trapped at local optima that are arbitrarily far from the global
optimum (Papadimitriou & Steiglitz, 1998).

## 4. Theory of Operation: The Joint Seed Search

### 4.1 Search Space and Fixed Parameters

The script `b26c_joint_seed_search.py` fixes sub-table seeds $s_3$ and $s_4$ to their current best values (`CRSCLTP3`
and `CRSCLTP4`, respectively) and exhaustively enumerates all $36 \times 36 = 1{,}296$ combinations of $s_1$ and $s_2$
drawn from the same 36-element candidate set used in B.22. The decision to fix $s_3$ and $s_4$ is justified by an
empirical observation from the B.22 sequential search: all 36 candidates for both Phase 3 and Phase 4 achieved
identical peak depths of 89,331, indicating that the solver's depth ceiling is insensitive to $s_3$ and $s_4$ at the
current operating point. Formally, the search computes:

$$
D^*(s_1, s_2) = \max_{(s_1, s_2) \in \mathcal{S} \times \mathcal{S}} D(s_1, s_2, \sigma_3^{\text{fixed}}, \sigma_4^{\text{fixed}})
$$

where $\mathcal{S}$ is the 36-element candidate set and $D(\cdot)$ is the peak depth achieved in a fixed-duration
decompression run.

### 4.2 Re-Compression Requirement

A subtle but critical methodological requirement distinguishes the B.26c search from a naive parameter sweep: each
candidate seed pair requires re-compression of the source file before decompression can be evaluated. The reason is that
the compressed `.crsce` file contains the LTP cross-sum vectors computed from the original matrix under a specific set
of seeds. If the decompressor uses different seeds (and hence a different cell-to-line mapping), the stored sums will be
inconsistent with the partition the decompressor constructs. The decompressor would then attempt to satisfy constraints
that no valid matrix can satisfy under the new partition, producing artificially inflated or deflated depth measurements.

The re-compression step ensures that for each candidate pair $(s_1, s_2)$, the compressed payload contains LTP sums
consistent with the partition defined by those seeds. The implementation proceeds as follows:

```python
# Step 1: Compress source_video with candidate seeds
compress_env["CRSCE_LTP_SEED_1"] = str(s1)
compress_env["CRSCE_LTP_SEED_2"] = str(s2)
compress_env["CRSCE_LTP_SEED_3"] = str(SEED3_FIXED)
compress_env["CRSCE_LTP_SEED_4"] = str(SEED4_FIXED)
compress_env["DISABLE_COMPRESS_DI"] = "1"  # Skip DI discovery for speed
subprocess.run([compress_bin, "-in", source_video, "-out", crsce_path], env=compress_env)

# Step 2: Decompress with the same seeds for `secs` seconds
decompress_env["CRSCE_LTP_SEED_1"] = str(s1)
decompress_env["CRSCE_LTP_SEED_2"] = str(s2)
# ... (same seeds as compression)
proc = subprocess.Popen([decompress_bin, "-in", crsce_path, "-out", out_path], env=decompress_env)
time.sleep(secs)
proc.send_signal(signal.SIGINT)  # Graceful termination for telemetry flush
```

The `DISABLE_COMPRESS_DI=1` environment variable skips the disambiguation index discovery phase during compression,
which would otherwise run the decompressor internally and dramatically increase per-candidate evaluation time. Since the
seed search is concerned only with peak decompression depth (a proxy for partition quality), not with producing a valid
compressed archive, DI discovery is unnecessary.

### 4.3 Fitness Metric: Peak DFS Depth

The fitness metric for each candidate pair is the peak DFS depth recorded in the decompressor's telemetry log during a
fixed-duration run. The decompressor writes structured JSON-L events to a file specified by the `CRSCE_EVENTS_PATH`
environment variable. Each event includes a `tags.depth` field recording the current DFS depth at the time of the
event. The script parses this log after the decompression run terminates and extracts the maximum depth:

```python
peak_depth = 0
for line in events_path.read_text().splitlines():
    entry = json.loads(line)
    d = entry.get("tags", {}).get("depth")
    if d is not None:
        peak_depth = max(peak_depth, int(d))
```

Peak depth is an appropriate fitness metric because the solver's primary bottleneck is the forcing dead zone in the
plateau band. A partition that enables deeper search within the same time budget does so because its constraint topology
produces more frequent and more effective forcing cascades, reducing the branching factor in the plateau region. In the
limit, a partition that allows the solver to traverse the entire matrix (depth $= s^2 = 261{,}121$) would yield
complete decompression. Every increment in peak depth therefore represents a measurable improvement in solver
effectiveness attributable to the partition structure.

### 4.4 Graceful Termination and Telemetry Fidelity

The script terminates each decompression run by sending `SIGINT` to the process after the configured number of seconds.
The decompressor is designed to handle `SIGINT` gracefully: it flushes all pending telemetry events to the JSON-L log
before exiting. If the process does not exit within 5 seconds of receiving `SIGINT`, the script escalates to `SIGKILL`
to prevent runaway processes from consuming resources:

```python
time.sleep(secs)
try:
    proc.send_signal(signal.SIGINT)
    proc.wait(timeout=5)
except (ProcessLookupError, subprocess.TimeoutExpired):
    proc.kill()
    proc.wait()
```

This two-stage termination protocol ensures that telemetry data is reliably captured under normal conditions while
providing a hard backstop against pathological decompressor behavior.

### 4.5 Progressive Output and Resumability

The joint search requires approximately 8.3 hours to complete at 20 seconds per decompression run plus approximately
3–5 seconds per re-compression. To guard against interrupted runs (hardware failures, user interruption, system
updates), the script writes results progressively to a JSON file after each candidate pair evaluation. The output
structure records the current best pair, overall progress, and the full list of evaluated results:

```python
summary = {
    "baseline": {"seed1": hex(SEED1_BASELINE), "seed2": hex(SEED2_BASELINE),
                 "depth": BASELINE_DEPTH},
    "best_found": best_pair,
    "progress": {"done": len(completed), "total": total_pairs},
    "results": all_results,
}
out_path.write_text(json.dumps(summary, indent=2))
```

The `--resume` flag allows restarting from a partial results file: previously evaluated pairs are loaded into a
dictionary keyed by `(seed1_hex, seed2_hex)` and skipped during the enumeration. This makes the search idempotent with
respect to interruption—each pair is evaluated at most once regardless of how many times the script is restarted.

## 5. Comparison with the Greedy Sequential Search

The relationship between `b26c_joint_seed_search.py` and `b22_seed_search.py` is that of an exhaustive verification
layer applied to the first two dimensions of a four-dimensional optimization that was previously solved by coordinate
descent.

The greedy search (`b22_seed_search.py`) operates in four sequential phases, each optimizing one seed while holding the
others fixed. Its computational cost is $4K = 144$ evaluations for $K = 36$ candidates, completing in approximately
$144 \times 50 = 7{,}200$ seconds (2 hours) at the B.22 default of 45 seconds per run. The joint search enumerates all
$K^2 = 1{,}296$ pairs for the first two sub-tables, requiring approximately $1{,}296 \times 23 \approx 29{,}808$
seconds (8.3 hours). The cost ratio is $1{,}296 / 72 = 18\times$ for the $(s_1, s_2)$ subspace, reflecting the
transition from $O(K)$ coordinate descent to $O(K^2)$ exhaustive search.

The critical question is whether this $18\times$ cost increase is justified. The answer depends on the degree of
coupling between sub-tables $T_0$ and $T_1$ in the plateau band. If the depth function $D(s_1, s_2)$ is approximately
separable—that is, $D(s_1, s_2) \approx f_1(s_1) + f_2(s_2) + C$ for some constant $C$—then the greedy sequential
optimum coincides with the joint optimum and the exhaustive search merely confirms what the greedy search already found.
If, on the other hand, $D$ exhibits significant interaction terms—situations where specific $(s_1, s_2)$ combinations
produce synergistic forcing cascades that neither seed achieves with arbitrary partners—then the exhaustive search may
discover strictly superior pairs that the greedy search cannot reach.

The B.22 observation that all 36 candidates for sub-tables $T_2$ and $T_3$ achieved identical depths of 89,331
suggests that the depth function is nearly constant in the $(s_3, s_4)$ dimensions at the current operating point. This
provides partial evidence for separability: if sub-tables 2 and 3 do not influence depth, they cannot interact with
sub-tables 0 and 1 either. However, this conclusion does not extend to the $(s_1, s_2)$ pair, where the B.22 sequential
search found measurable depth differences across candidates. The joint search resolves this ambiguity empirically.

## 6. Algorithmic Complexity and Runtime Analysis

### 6.1 Per-Candidate Cost Model

Each candidate pair evaluation consists of two phases: re-compression and decompression. Let $t_c$ denote the
re-compression time and $t_d$ the configured decompression window. The total per-candidate wall time is:

$$
t_{\text{eval}} = t_c + t_d + t_{\text{overhead}}
$$

where $t_{\text{overhead}}$ accounts for process startup, telemetry parsing, and file I/O. In practice, $t_c \approx
3\text{--}5$ seconds, $t_d = 20$ seconds (the script default), and $t_{\text{overhead}} < 1$ second, yielding
$t_{\text{eval}} \approx 23\text{--}25$ seconds.

### 6.2 Total Runtime

The total number of evaluations is $|\mathcal{S}|^2 = 36^2 = 1{,}296$. Total wall time is:

$$
T_{\text{total}} = 1{,}296 \times t_{\text{eval}} \approx 1{,}296 \times 23 = 29{,}808 \text{ seconds} \approx 8.3 \text{ hours}
$$

This is a sequential computation: each candidate pair evaluation requires exclusive access to the compressor and
decompressor binaries. Parallelization would require either multiple builds or careful process isolation with separate
temporary directories, neither of which the current script implements. The sequential design is deliberate: it avoids
resource contention that could introduce measurement noise into the depth metric.

### 6.3 Search Space Coverage

The 36-element candidate set provides coverage of the single-byte suffix space of the `CRSCLTP` prefix. The full
64-bit seed space contains $2^{64} \approx 1.8 \times 10^{19}$ possible values, of which 36 represents an
infinitesimal fraction. However, the structured seed format ensures that all candidates produce well-formed LCG
initial states (no degenerate seeds such as zero), and the 36-element set has been empirically validated through the
B.22 campaign to contain seeds spanning the observed depth range. Expanding the candidate set to, say, the full
printable ASCII range (95 characters, yielding $95^2 = 9{,}025$ pairs) would increase runtime to approximately 58
hours—still feasible but approaching the limit of practicality for a sequential search.

## 7. Expected Outcomes

### 7.1 Baseline Performance

The greedy sequential search identified the pair $(s_1, s_2) = (\texttt{CRSCLTP0}, \texttt{CRSCLTPG})$ with a peak
depth of 89,331. This serves as the baseline against which all 1,296 joint-search candidates are compared. Each
evaluated pair's depth is compared against this baseline, and the script flags any pair that exceeds it.

### 7.2 Optimistic Scenario

If significant interaction effects exist between sub-tables $T_0$ and $T_1$, the joint search may discover a pair
$(s_1^*, s_2^*)$ with depth exceeding 89,331 by a meaningful margin (e.g., $\geq 500$ cells, the directional
significance threshold established in B.22.3). Such a finding would demonstrate that coordinate descent is insufficient
for seed optimization and that future campaigns should employ joint search or more sophisticated optimization
strategies. It would also immediately improve the production CRSCE solver's performance at zero algorithmic cost—the
new seeds would simply replace the old ones in the source code constants.

### 7.3 Confirmatory Scenario

If the greedy sequential optimum is also the joint optimum (or within the noise floor of the depth metric), the joint
search provides valuable negative evidence: the $(s_1, s_2)$ coupling is weak, and the $O(K)$ greedy search is
sufficient for this subspace. This would validate the separability assumption and support extending the greedy
methodology to larger candidate sets or higher-dimensional searches (e.g., varying the LCG multiplier or increment
rather than just the seed suffix).

### 7.4 Landscape Characterization

Beyond identifying the optimal pair, the full $36 \times 36$ results matrix provides a two-dimensional landscape of
the depth function $D(s_1, s_2)$. Structural patterns in this landscape—clusters of high-performing pairs, correlation
with seed suffix distance, symmetry between $s_1$ and $s_2$—would inform the design of future partition optimization
strategies. For instance, if the landscape is smooth (nearby seeds produce similar depths), local search methods such
as simulated annealing or evolutionary strategies could efficiently explore larger seed spaces. If it is rugged (small
seed changes produce large depth swings), exhaustive enumeration or random sampling may be the only reliable
approaches.

## 8. Experimental Results

### 8.1 Search Execution

The joint search completed all 1,296 candidate pairs in approximately 9 hours using the `llvm-release` preset on an
Apple Silicon host. Per-pair wall time averaged approximately 23–25 seconds: approximately 3–5 seconds for
re-compression and 20 seconds for decompression. Results were written progressively to `tools/b26c_results.json`
after each pair evaluation, providing a full record of the depth landscape.

### 8.2 Winner: CRSCLTPV + CRSCLTPP at Depth 91,090

The exhaustive search identified the seed pair $(\sigma_1, \sigma_2) = (\texttt{CRSCLTPV}, \texttt{CRSCLTPP})$ as
the joint optimum, achieving a peak DFS depth of **91,090**. This surpasses the greedy sequential baseline of 89,331
by **1,759 cells**, an improvement of **+1.97%**. The result confirms the optimistic scenario described in Section 7.2:
significant interaction effects exist between sub-tables $T_0$ and $T_1$, and the coordinate-wise greedy search was
trapped at a local optimum in the joint seed space.

The winning pair uses a seed1 value (`CRSCLTPV`, suffix byte `0x56`) that does not appear in the greedy sequential
Phase-1 ranking: when seed2 was fixed to the default `CRSCLTP1`–`CRSCLTP4` values, `CRSCLTPV` performed below the
observed Phase-1 winner `CRSCLTP0`. The joint search recovered it because `CRSCLTPV` is synergistic with
`CRSCLTPP`—a combination that the greedy search, conditioned on the Phase-1 winner, could not discover.

### 8.3 Depth Landscape Statistics

The full 1,296-pair landscape has the following structure:

| Metric                    | Value            |
|---------------------------|------------------|
| Total pairs evaluated     | 1,296            |
| Mean depth                | 86,941           |
| Minimum depth             | 84,265           |
| Maximum depth (winner)    | 91,090           |
| Pairs ≥ 89,331 (baseline) | 29 (2.2%)        |
| Pairs ≥ 90,000            | 7 (0.54%)        |
| Pairs ≥ 91,000            | 1 (0.08%)        |

The landscape is highly non-uniform: 97.8% of pairs fall below the greedy baseline, and only 2.2% exceed it. This
confirms the rugged landscape hypothesis from Section 7.4—small changes in seed suffix produce large depth swings,
making gradient-free exhaustive search the appropriate strategy for this problem. The winner at 91,090 is a clear
outlier at the top of the distribution.

The ten highest-performing pairs identified by the search are:

The greedy sequential pair `(CRSCLTP0, CRSCLTPG)` scored 89,331 and does not appear in the top 10. Seven pairs
exceed 90,000, and the top pair exceeds second place by 264 cells—a gap comparable to the entire improvement seen
in the B.22 greedy search campaign.

### 8.5 Confirmed Interaction Effects

The results definitively confirm that seed1 and seed2 are not independent. Under the separability assumption, the
greedy sequential optimum would be the joint optimum, and the distribution of joint pair scores would be
approximately the product of the independent marginals. Instead:

1. **The greedy sequential pair ranks 29th** (89,331 depth) out of 1,296 pairs evaluated — it is not the joint
   optimum.
2. **The joint winner (CRSCLTPV) was not the greedy Phase-1 winner** (CRSCLTP0). The value of seed1 is
   context-dependent: `CRSCLTPV` is superior when paired with `CRSCLTPP`, but this combination was invisible
   to the greedy search which fixed seed2 at the wrong default during Phase 1.
3. **The landscape is rugged**: the coefficient of variation across depths is approximately 1.4%, but the
   range is 6,825 cells (84,265 to 91,090) — a 7.6% spread relative to the mean.

These findings validate the local optimum hypothesis from Section 3.2 and establish that joint optimization of
the $(s_1, s_2)$ pair provides a material gain that coordinate descent cannot achieve.

### 8.6 Deployment and Validation

The new seeds were deployed in `src/Decompress/Solvers/LtpTable.cpp`:

```cpp
constexpr std::uint64_t kSeed1 = 0x4352'5343'4C54'5056ULL;  // CRSCLTPV
constexpr std::uint64_t kSeed2 = 0x4352'5343'4C54'5050ULL;  // CRSCLTPP
```

End-to-end validation via the 30-minute `uselessMachine` test confirmed the depth of **91,090** under real workload
conditions, matching the 20-second B.26c search result. The existing test suite (30 unit and integration tests)
passed without modification; the new seeds change the DI values for stored `.crsce` archives but the round-trip test
re-compresses and re-decompresses end-to-end, making it self-consistent under any seed configuration.

## 9. Conclusion

The `b26c_joint_seed_search.py` script provided definitive evidence that the B.22 greedy sequential search for LTP
partition seeds was trapped at a local optimum. By exhaustively enumerating all 1,296 combinations of sub-table seeds
$s_1$ and $s_2$ with matched re-compression, the search identified the pair $(\texttt{CRSCLTPV}, \texttt{CRSCLTPP})$
achieving depth 91,090—a 1,759-cell (1.97%) improvement over the prior greedy result of 89,331.

Three conclusions follow from this work. First, the depth function $D(s_1, s_2)$ over the 36-element candidate set
is non-separable: the optimal seed1 depends on the choice of seed2, and vice versa. Coordinate-wise optimization
missed the global optimum because the winning seed1 value (`CRSCLTPV`) ranked poorly when paired with the defaults
used during Phase 1. Second, the landscape is rugged: the 7.6% depth range (84,265–91,090) across 1,296 pairs
precludes gradient-based or local search strategies; exhaustive enumeration is necessary to guarantee optimality
within the candidate set. Third, the re-compression methodology introduced in B.22 is essential for measurement
validity—naive parameter sweeps without re-compression would produce artificially inflated or deflated depth scores
due to LTP sum mismatch between the stored payload and the active partition.

The methodological lesson generalizes: any system that optimizes structured parameters via coordinate descent over a
non-smooth objective should verify the result with a pairwise (or higher-order) exhaustive check in the most
sensitive dimensions, even at significant computational cost. In the CRSCE case, the 18× cost increase from $4K=144$
greedy evaluations to $K^2=1{,}296$ joint evaluations produced a 1.97% solver improvement that would otherwise have
been permanently foregone.
