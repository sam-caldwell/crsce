# A Binary Benford Gradient and Payload-Derived Probabilistic Priors for CRSCE Decompression

Sam Caldwell

---

## Abstract

Benford's Law establishes that naturally occurring numerical data exhibits a non-uniform distribution of leading significant digits. This paper proposes and formalizes an analogous phenomenon for binary data — the Binary Benford Gradient — and demonstrates that its effects are detectable within the compressed payload of the Cross-Sums Compression and Expansion (CRSCE) algorithm without any prior knowledge of the source data format. A payload-derived self-calibrating prior layer (BPP) is proposed as a decompressor-side heuristic that exploits this gradient to improve cell determination during Phase II search. The approach requires no modification to the CRSCE payload format and imposes no assumption about source file type, making it applicable to the general case. An experimental validation program is outlined that tests the gradient's detectability from stored cross-sum values, its magnitude across natural data classes, and its effect on the 583-cell structural residual observed in experiment B.63h (Caldwell, 2026).

## 1. Benford's Law as a Motivating Framework

Newcomb (1881) first observed that logarithm tables were more worn at the front than the back, suggesting that smaller leading digits appear more frequently in natural numerical data. Benford (1938) formalized and extended this observation across dozens of naturally occurring datasets, deriving the distribution P(d) = log₁₀(1 + 1/d) for leading decimal digit d. Hill (1995) provided the definitive theoretical explanation: Benford's Law emerges inevitably from any distribution that is scale-invariant, that is, invariant under multiplication by an arbitrary positive constant. When data spans multiple orders of magnitude and arises from multiplicative generating processes — population counts, financial transactions, physical measurements — scale invariance holds approximately, and Benford's distribution follows.

Benford's Law is not invoked here as a mechanism to be applied directly to binary data. It is presented as a motivating structural insight: that real-world data, even when aggregated across diverse domains and formats, is not a uniform random process at the level of its most significant representations. Structure persists at the digit or bit level, and that structure is derivable from first principles rather than requiring knowledge of the specific source. The question this paper addresses is whether an analogous gradient exists for the most significant bits of naturally occurring byte-width binary data, and whether that gradient is recoverable from the CRSCE payload itself without format-specific knowledge.

## 2. The Binary Benford Gradient: Theoretical Derivation

For an unsigned byte value x drawn from a distribution F over [0, 255], the probability that the most significant bit (MSB, bit position 7) equals 0 is precisely P(x < 128) = F(127). For F to assign P(MSB = 0) = 0.5, it must be uniform over [0, 255]. Any distribution concentrated below 128 — that is, any distribution whose median is below the midpoint of the byte range — produces P(MSB = 0) > 0.5.

The more general claim is that P(bit k = 0) is a monotonically decreasing function of k from k = 0 (LSB) to k = 7 (MSB). This follows from the observation that for a distribution concentrated in the lower portion of [0, 255], higher-order bits transition from 0 to 1 less frequently than lower-order bits: bit 7 transitions once across the full byte range, bit 6 transitions twice, bit 5 four times, and so on. A distribution that is approximately log-uniform over its support — the binary analogue of the scale invariance that produces Benford's Law in decimal — produces a gradient across bit positions that is computable from the support bounds alone. Formally, if x is log-uniformly distributed over [a, b] with 0 < a < b ≤ 255, the probability that bit k equals 0 is:

P(bit k = 0) = Σ F([2j · 2^k, (2j+1) · 2^k − 1]) for j = 0, 1, 2, ...

where the sum ranges over intervals where bit k is 0. This quantity decreases monotonically with k for distributions concentrated below 128, establishing the Binary Benford Gradient as a theoretical prediction for naturally occurring unsigned byte data.

The gradient is not universal. Two's complement signed integers near zero, floating-point exponent fields, and cryptographically generated bytes are counterexamples where the gradient is absent or inverted. This delimits the claim precisely: the Binary Benford Gradient characterizes naturally occurring, non-negative, non-cryptographic byte data arising from processes with approximately log-uniform generating distributions. Crucially, the decompressor need not know which regime applies — the self-calibrating mechanism described in Section 4 infers this from the payload directly.

## 3. The Gradient Is Detectable from the CRSCE Payload

The central theoretical contribution of this paper is that the Binary Benford Gradient, if present in the source data, leaves a detectable signature in the CRSCE compressed payload — specifically in the stored row cross-sum values (LSM) — without requiring any access to the uncompressed source. This follows from the deterministic structure of the CSM row-major mapping.

At dimension s = 127, the CSM is populated row-major from the source byte stream. Because 127 is not divisible by 8, each row begins at a different intra-byte bit position. Row r begins at source bit offset 127r, which falls at bit position (127r) mod 8 within its byte. Each row therefore contains a deterministic, computable count of cells at each bit position 0 through 7, and this count varies from row to row by a fixed, format-independent amount determined solely by the dimension s.

Denote by m_k(r) the number of cells in row r that fall at bit position k. If the Binary Benford Gradient holds — that is, if P(bit k = 1) increases monotonically with k — then the expected row sum for row r is:

E[LSM(r)] = Σ_k m_k(r) · P(bit k = 1)

which is a weighted sum of per-position one-probabilities, with weights determined entirely by the row-major mapping. Rows with a higher proportion of MSB-position cells will, under the gradient hypothesis, have systematically lower expected LSM values. This prediction is testable directly from the stored LSM values in the CRSCE payload using a linear regression of LSM(r) against the vector of bit-position counts m_k(r) across all rows. A statistically significant negative coefficient on the MSB-position count m_7(r) confirms that the gradient is payload-detectable.

This is a materially stronger result than a corpus-level measurement of bit-position frequencies. It demonstrates that the CRSCE constraint system implicitly encodes statistical fingerprints of the source data's generating distribution, and that those fingerprints are recoverable by the decompressor without any external information. The compressed representation is not informationally opaque with respect to source statistics — it is translucent in a precise, measurable sense — and that translucency is exploitable without modifying a single bit of the payload format.

## 4. The Self-Calibrating Byte-Position Prior Layer

The payload-detectable gradient supports a self-calibrating Byte-Position Prior (BPP) layer operating as a pre-Phase I processing stage in the CRSCE decompressor. The BPP layer proceeds in two steps.

First, the decompressor fits the linear regression of LSM(r) against bit-position count vectors m_k(r) for k = 0 through 7 across all s rows of the current block. The regression coefficients estimate the per-position one-probabilities P(bit k = 1), calibrated to the specific block being decompressed rather than to an external corpus. If the block derives from source data following the gradient, the regression yields an increasing sequence of coefficients from k = 0 to k = 7, confirming the gradient's presence and quantifying its magnitude. If the block derives from maximally entropic data — encrypted payload or true random bytes — the regression coefficients converge to approximately 0.5 uniformly, and the BPP layer correctly assigns flat priors, contributing nothing and causing no harm. The self-calibration is therefore a diagnostic as much as an inference: it tells the decompressor whether the source belongs to the natural-data regime where the gradient is exploitable.

Second, the fitted per-position probabilities are mapped to per-cell priors w(i) = P(cell i = 1), where cell i's bit position is (i mod 8). These priors are consumed by the Phase II DFS in a weighted restart ordering: each restart samples cell ordering proportionally to prior uncertainty, prioritizing cells far from 0.5 — in either direction — for early assignment. This retains the macroscopic diversity that produced the B.63e breakthrough (Caldwell, 2026), where random orderings outperformed most-constrained-first by 15.5 percentage points by avoiding premature commitment to locally optimal decisions. The BPP layer adds a structured gradient that steers the restart ensemble toward the region of the search space most consistent with the block's inferred source statistics, while preserving the ensemble breadth that makes random restarts effective.

Critically, the BPP layer does not perform hard pre-seeding of cells from the prior. A probabilistic prior of P(cell = 0) = 0.85, however well-founded, is not a certainty. A single incorrect forced assignment propagates through IntBound into cascade constraint violations, as the B.63e results demonstrate via the inverse: a 32-fold amplification factor means one wrong seed can misdetermine hundreds of cells. The prior therefore governs ordering only. Every cell assignment remains driven by constraint propagation or DFS branching, never by the prior alone. This preserves the solver's correctness guarantee unconditionally.

## 5. Experimental Validation Program

Proof of the Binary Benford Gradient and its CRSCE application proceeds through three experiments that build sequentially on existing experimental infrastructure.

**Experiment 1: Corpus-level gradient measurement.** Across a corpus spanning UTF-8 natural language text, structured binary formats (ELF, MP4, PDF, PNG), integer sensor data, and audio PCM samples, compute P(bit k = 0) for k = 0 through 7 at the byte level. The null hypothesis is P(bit k = 0) = 0.5 for all k; rejection across corpus categories at a chosen significance level establishes the gradient empirically. A monotonicity test on the eight estimated probabilities — the Jonckheere-Terpstra test for ordered alternatives (Jonckheere, 1954) — determines whether the gradient is consistently ordered from LSB to MSB. The corpus must explicitly include already-compressed and encrypted data as negative controls to confirm that the gradient is specific to natural data and not a universal artifact. This experiment is executable in Python against publicly available corpora and establishes the general phenomenon independently of CRSCE.

**Experiment 2: Payload-detectability test.** For the 1,331-block MP4 test file used throughout the B-series experiments (Caldwell, 2026), fit the linear regression of LSM(r) against bit-position count vectors m_k(r) for each block independently. Report the distribution of regression coefficients across blocks, the proportion of blocks where the gradient direction is correctly recovered, and the correlation between regression-inferred gradient magnitude and actual bit-position frequencies computed from ground-truth source bytes. A significant positive correlation confirms that LSM values carry recoverable gradient information. This experiment requires no new compression runs — it operates entirely on stored payload data from existing experiments and uncompressed source bytes available from the test file.

**Experiment 3: BPP-augmented restart census.** Modify the B.63e random restart architecture to use BPP-weighted ordering as described in Section 4. Run a 10,001-restart census on the same 50%-density blocks as B.63h and compare the distribution of peak determination values using a two-sample Kolmogorov-Smirnov test against the B.63h baseline. The operationally meaningful outcome is whether any restart penetrates the 583-cell floor. A reduction in mean residual size, even without full elimination, constitutes a positive result and motivates further investigation into whether additional constraint configuration changes — combined with BPP ordering — can close the residual entirely.

## 6. Implications for the 583-Cell Structural Residual

The 583-cell residual established in B.63h resists all tested search strategies, including 10,001 random restarts, CDCL, and beam search (Caldwell, 2026). Section 10.3 of the CRSCE paper identifies this residual as information-theoretic: the payload's 1,804-bit deficit below the Shannon limit means the stored constraints are insufficient to uniquely determine all cells, regardless of solver sophistication (Shannon, 1948).

The BPP layer does not challenge this analysis. It does not claim to recover information that the payload does not contain. Its claim is narrower and more defensible: that the payload contains implicit statistical information about the source's generating distribution — the Binary Benford Gradient — that the current solver discards, and that exploiting this implicit information improves the ordering of Phase II search in a way that reaches deeper determination chains on natural data. Whether this improvement penetrates the 583-cell floor is an empirical question that Experiment 3 answers. The theoretical expectation is modest: the floor is information-theoretic, not ordering-dependent, and the BPP layer addresses ordering. However, the B.63e results demonstrated that ordering effects account for a 15.5 percentage point range in determination outcomes, and it is not excluded that better ordering closes a portion of the residual by reaching cascade chains that no prior ordering sampled.

For maximally entropic data, the BPP layer self-identifies as uninformative and degrades gracefully to existing random restart behavior. CRSCE's source-independent compression ratio claim is therefore unaffected. The BPP layer adds a practical improvement for the overwhelming majority of real-world inputs without disturbing the algorithm's theoretical properties on adversarial inputs.

## 7. Conclusion

This paper has proposed the Binary Benford Gradient as a theoretical regularity of naturally occurring byte-width binary data, derived its detectability from the CRSCE compressed payload without format-specific knowledge, and specified a self-calibrating Byte-Position Prior layer that exploits this gradient to improve Phase II decompression search. The proposal is conservative in its claims — it requires no payload format changes, no external corpus, no file format detection, and no modification to the solver's correctness guarantee — while being specific enough to be experimentally falsifiable through the three-experiment validation program described in Section 5.

The broader implication extends beyond CRSCE. The payload-detectability result of Section 3 establishes that constraint-based compressed representations are not informationally opaque with respect to their source statistics. Cross-sum projections implicitly encode properties of the source's generating distribution, and decompressors that exploit this implicit information are strictly more capable than those that treat the payload as a pure constraint system. The Binary Benford Gradient is the first identified instance of this phenomenon in CRSCE research, but the general principle — that projection-based summaries of natural data carry recoverable statistical fingerprints of their source — suggests a broader research program at the intersection of information theory, constraint satisfaction, and empirical data statistics.
